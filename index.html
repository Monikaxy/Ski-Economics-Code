The Snow Still Falls, But the Ski Economy Is Starting to Shift

March 4, 2026

Monika Xiao

When the Slopes Don’t Match the Plan

The third day of Thanksgiving was the busiest day for ski patrol. The High Noon run at Steamboat Ski Resort was packed with skiers, from speedsters to those who had never skied an intermediate run before. For these skiers, whose skill level wasn't up to the intermediate level, this wasn't overconfidence, but rather the only option on the mountain.

Colorado is one of the most popular ski destinations in the United States. Charlie He and his friends, traveling from New York State, booked their Thanksgiving and Christmas trip to Colorado a month in advance, along with ski-in-and-out hotels, which made the trip a considerable expense. As a longtime skiing enthusiast, Charlie said the excellent snow conditions and experience he had at Steamboat last year led him to plan another early-season trip. However, the snow conditions this season caught him off guard.

"I didn't expect that only one run would be open," Charlie said. "If I had known this would happen, I wouldn't have chosen to come to Colorado."

Charlie's friend Kasper Li, after finding the intermediate slopes too difficult and being bumped into by another skier, ultimately called the ski patrol back to his original spot. Many other beginners like Kasper struggled downhill and eventually sought help from the ski patrol. The ski resort had a banner at the lowest chairlift that read No Beginner Area, but for destination skiers, not taking that chairlift meant changing their plans.

After careful consideration, Charlie and his companions cancelled their Christmas trip because, being from New York, they felt the East Coast ski resorts were experiencing a much better season than usual.

Turning East for Better Snow

In previous years, the East Coast's snow conditions were typically less favorable compared to West Coast ski resorts. However, this year, with overall unstable snow conditions, East Coast resorts actually had an advantage. Killington, located in Vermont, in the eastern United States, had better snow conditions than usual this year. However, even larger ski resorts like Killington still relied on artificial snowmaking to operate at the beginning of the season. Against this backdrop, those working in the ski industry are also undergoing changes.

Working Through an Unpredictable Season

John Hobbs, a 54-year-old part-time ski instructor in Killington, has been in the industry for three decades. He also used to run an organization that certifies coaches and instructors on the East Coast. Skiing has always been a big part of his life. Currently, he works as a plumber; in the winter, he usually only works as a plumber about two days a week and as a ski instructor four days a week. In the summer, he goes back to full-time plumbing.

Regarding this year's ski season, although there were some facility issues at the beginning, such as the newly installed magic carpet conveyor belt in the beginner area being unstable, overall, the snow conditions this year have been good, which has brought more job opportunities. John Hobbs said that his workload this season is significantly higher than in previous years.

“I’ve definitely had more work this year than I’ve had in the past. Having good snow conditions is helpful, with the West Coast not having great conditions, I think that’s helped us a lot too.” He believes this is not only due to the better snow conditions in his area but also related to the poor snow conditions in some ski areas on the West Coast, which has led some skiers to switch to the East Coast.

When discussing the impact of the recent instability in snow conditions on his work, John Hobbs said that when only some runs are open at the beginning of the ski season, there is definitely a decrease in visitor traffic and business. "There's definitely a little less traffic from the public, a little less business.”

However, he also believes that this is not entirely unexpected in the early season, as being able to open some runs in November is already considered a good situation. Compared to some surrounding ski resorts that may not be open at all, being able to ski in itself means that the ski season has begun.

Ski Tourism shift under pressure

Killington relies on a large-scale artificial snow-making system and a mature snow-making infrastructure, which often enables it to maintain relatively good snow conditions in the early season. However, from a broader industry perspective, climate change is gradually altering the operation mode of the skiing industry. Although large ski resorts still have certain coping capabilities, smaller and lower elevation ski resorts are more vulnerable to unstable snow conditions.

The uncertainty of snow conditions has also changed the behavior of tourists, resulting in decreased satisfaction and loyalty, reduced number of skiers, shortened stay time, and even for some ski resorts in Colorado, some tourists have begun to choose other skiing destinations. With the slowdown of skiing tourism, the economies and ecological environments of many regions that rely on this industry have also been affected, especially for Colorado this year. To cope with these changes, some ski resorts have begun to rely on technical means such as artificial snow-making, to extend the duration of the ski season as much as possible.

According to data from the National Ski Areas Association, from 1991 to 2022, reported natural snowfall at U.S. ski areas generally declined, an average annual decrease of about 0.27%. At the same time, the ski season is gradually shortening, and peak snowfall is occurring earlier. Research shows that even considering the addition of snowmaking techniques, since the past half century, the ski season in the four major U.S. ski areas has shortened by an average of about 5 days. Senior Associate at Lynker Technologies Dr.Cameron Wobus has also analyzed the timing and impact of ski opening dates. By 2050, his team estimates the end of the season could move more than 20 days.

A Changing Future for the Ski Economy

Environmental economist Dr. Nejem Raheem stated that these short-term changes have ripple effects on local and regional economies.

“What happens is because you have this large, complex seasonal economy that’s dependent on it; many people make the decision to go to these places ahead of time, and they can’t really change their plans very easily.” Dr. Raheem also believes that while the instability of a single ski season may not significantly impact tourism in the short term, in the long term, this persistent climate instability will disrupt the current balance of the ski economy.

“My educated guess is that if the changes are not permanent, the disruption will be even worse, because it’ll become like a rolling, chaotic attempt to constantly try to adjust.” Dr. Raheem predicts that if this climate unpredictability persists, ski resorts will be unable to adapt and adjust in time. Decades later, this could lead to operational difficulties or even closures for many smaller ski resorts in the United States, or those in lower latitudes, thereby affecting those who depend on the industry for their livelihoods.

Even in these circumstances, people who love skiing and their profession won't easily give up.

John Hobbs maintains a certain optimism regarding the long-term uncertainties brought about by climate change in the ski industry. Hobbs says he certainly thinks about the future, but he won't leave the industry because of one or two ski seasons of change.

For John Hobbs, who has spent more than half his life working in the ski industry, skiing has always been something worth persisting in. Even as winters become increasingly unpredictable, he still believes the ski season will come and still chooses to return to the mountains every year.

But for the entire ski industry, changes are already beginning to appear. As snowfall becomes more unpredictable, ski resorts, tourists, and local economies that depend on the ski season are all being forced to adapt to the new reality. The snow hasn't disappeared, but the ski tourism economy built around snow is entering a more uncertain era.


“There is always a second doubt, but I've stuck with it for thirty-something years already, so I doubt I'm leaving.”

- John Hobbs
